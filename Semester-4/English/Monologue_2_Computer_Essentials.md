## Step 1. Introduction

Bill Gates once said: "The computer was born to solve problems that did not exist before." I think this is funny but also true. Today we use computers for everything — studying, working, playing games. ***Firstly***, I will explain how computers work. ***Moreover***, I will talk about the history of computing. ***Finally***, I will discuss supercomputers and how they are different from normal PCs.

## Step 2. How do computers work?

2.1. ***To begin with***, **to accomplish a task** on a computer, you need three things: hardware, software, and input. Hardware is the physical part — the monitor, **keyboard and mouse**, **scanner and printer**, **headphones and a microphone**, and **speakers**. Inside the computer, the **motherboard** has the main chips that **make up the central processing unit**, and also the **hard drive**, **RAM**, and **graphics card**. Software is the programs that you **install on the computer**, like the operating system or **application software**.

2.2. When you start a computer, a process called **booting up** begins. You **turn on a hardware device**, and the system loads the programs. Then you **provide the input** — you can do it by **clicking on the icon** or **entering a command**. The hardware **receives commands from computer software** and does the work. ***For instance***, when you want to open a website, you type a **URL** in the browser. If you need to print something, the computer will **send the information from your computer over the cable to the printer**.

## Step 3. History of computing

3.1. The history of computers is very interesting. The first computers were **cumbersome** and used **to take up entire rooms**. ***However***, people kept improving them. Konrad Zuse is **credited with inventing the computer** — he managed **to create the first freely programmable mechanical computing device** in 1936. His machine needed **to have a memory to store results of each step** and **to keep track of the many results**. ***Furthermore***, during World War II, the ENIAC computer was built **to use vacuum tubes instead of electric motors and levers**. This helped **to speed up calculations** and **compute numbers** much faster. Over the years, computers **evolved from a cumbersome and slow device to a quick and smart machine** with **incredible processing power**. Now they can easily **fit on one's desktop** or even in a pocket.

3.2. ***As for*** my own PC, I built it myself. It has an AMD Ryzen 7 processor, 32 GB of RAM, and an NVIDIA RTX 4070 graphics card. I use it **to perform calculations** and for tasks ***such as*** programming, cybersecurity practice, and gaming.

## Step 4. Supercomputers

4.1. A supercomputer is a very powerful machine **capable of crunching a complex problem** that a normal computer cannot solve. Like any computer, it **takes in information by input**, processes it, and **generates output**. ***Nevertheless***, the main difference is that supercomputers **use parallel processing instead of the serial processing** that regular computers use.

4.2. ***In other words***, a normal computer can only **do one thing at a time**, but a supercomputer can **do many things at a time**. It works by **splitting a problem into chunks** and giving each chunk to a different processor. This way it **uses much of processing power** very efficiently. You can also **upgrade** a computer **with a better processor and more memory** or **double the memory capacity** to make it faster. The **development of transistors and integrated circuits** made modern supercomputers possible. ***For example***, the IBM 701 in 1953 was **the first off-the-shelf supercomputer**, and IBM's Deep Blue beat chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997.

## Step 6. Conclusion

***To sum up***, computers work by combining hardware, software, and input. ***Additionally***, the history of computing shows how machines went from huge rooms to small devices in our pockets. Supercomputers solve the hardest problems using parallel processing. ***In conclusion***, new technologies like quantum computing will keep pushing the limits of what computers can do.
